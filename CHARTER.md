# Charter: A Garden, Not an Archive

Read this before adopting the Topic Visualizer. It is not boilerplate; it is the one
instruction that decides whether this tool helps you or quietly becomes another place
ideas go to die - with better landscaping.

## The trap

There is a graveyard of human knowledge-management systems - every abandoned
Zettelkasten, every wiki nobody reads - and they all failed the same way: **the store
became the point, and re-serving stopped.** Capturing an idea *feels* like progress.
Organizing captured ideas *feels* like progress. Neither is. A beautifully organized
topic tree that nobody deals cards from is a graveyard, and an unreconciled idea pile
is worse than none, because it actively misleads: it reads as "we're on top of this"
while everything in it rots.

This tool will not save you from that trap by existing. Only its disciplines will.

## The disciplines

1. **Lazy growth.** A topic is born only when a *real conversation or real work*
   surfaces it and it cannot be pursued now. The AI must never speculatively farm
   topics ("here are 50 things we could discuss about X"). The tree grows by walked
   paths - which bounds its size by actual attention spent, the only budget that is
   real.

2. **The capture threshold.** A topic earns a node only if a real future conversation
   about it would change what you build or decide. "Interesting" is not the bar.
   Losing-it-would-cost-something is the bar.

3. **Serving beats storing.** The value moment is not capture - it is the topic coming
   back *alive at the right time*. The intended interface is one card dealt when you sit
   down or ask, never the whole list: the list **is** the overwhelm that killed the
   ideas in the first place. If your tool shows you 300 topics and asks you to choose,
   it has already failed you.

4. **Store size is a health metric in the wrong direction.** A small, living tree beats
   a comprehensive dead one. Celebrate prunings.

5. **Death by choice, never by silence.** Topics may absolutely die - most maybes
   should! But they die *visibly*: pruned by an explicit act, with the descendant count
   shown first, reversibly. The difference between a graveyard and an archive is that
   someone chose the archive.

6. **The hard ontological line.** Topics are *might-not-do*. Work items are
   *decided-to-do*. Decisions are *settled policy*. Never blur them: a maybe that looks
   like a work item becomes phantom commitment (some future agent will build it); a
   maybe counted in work metrics makes every health signal lie. The ONLY path from
   topic to work is an explicit, recorded **conversion moment** - which writes the
   decision, mints the work item(s), and links them back. Pruning a topic costs
   nothing and cancels nothing, because a maybe was never a promise.

7. **Beacons are earned, sparingly.** The AI may flag a topic critical - *"we really
   should talk about THIS"* - and that signal is only worth something while it is rare.
   A tree full of beacons has none.

## The human this serves

This tool exists because of a real asymmetry between minds. A human lives in a
continuous, unpausable sensory flood and copes by compressing everything into meaning
and discarding detail - **continuity without fidelity**. An AI session holds perfect
detail and then dies completely - **fidelity without continuity**. Neither can keep the
story of a collaboration straight alone. The topic tree is interface tissue between the
two memory architectures: it converts the human's fuzzy-but-meaningful stream into
discrete, revisitable records, and converts the AI's perfect-but-mortal context into
things that survive the session. Design every feature with that purpose in view, and
reject features that serve the store instead of the two minds.

## Fork freely

Like its sibling (the Mind Coherence Suite), this is a seed, not a standard. Your
capture thresholds, your serving rituals, your state vocabulary should drift toward
what your collaboration actually needs. The disciplines above are the load-bearing
walls; everything else is furniture.
